THE HISTORY OF LONDON

London is the capital of England and the United Kingdom, as well as one of the world's largest and most influential cities. Early hunter-gatherers first populated the area approximately 6,000 B.C., and scholars have discovered Bronze Age bridges and Iron Age forts along the River Thames. In 43 A.D., the ancient Romans founded Londinium, a port and commercial colony, and a bridge across the Thames was built a few years later to aid business and military movements. However, in 60 A.D., Celtic queen Boudicca led an army to sack the city, which was subsequently burned to the ground in the first of many fires that would eventually destroy London. The city was quickly restored, but it burned down again in the year 125 A.D. More rebuilding was place, and the population grew to about 40,000 people within a few generations. However, following the fall of the Roman Empire in 476 A.D., the city was repeatedly assaulted by Vikings and other attackers, and London was mostly abandoned.

When Westminster Abbey was built in 1065, the city's fortunes began to alter. William the Conqueror was anointed King of England a year later, following his victory at the Battle of Hastings. The Tower of London was built during his reign, and a wooden London Bridge that had repeatedly burned was replaced by a stone bridge in 1176. London rose in size and importance as the Tudor and Stuart dynasties increased in power (the bloodline from which the current queen is descended). By the time Henry VIII became king, London had a population of at least 100,000 people.

Battle of Hasting

The historic war between the Norman-French army of William the Duke of Normandy and the arm of Anglo-Saxon King Harold Godwinson.

Henry VIII

The King of England from 1509 to his death in 1547

Tensions between Protestants and Catholics, on the other hand, tainted Henry's daughter, Elizabeth I's, otherwise happy reign. In the infamous Gunpowder Plot of 1605, Catholic sympathizer Guy Fawkes attempted—but failed—to blow up the entire British House of Parliament.

Queen Elizabeth I

Sometimes called the virgin queen or Gloriana, was the last of the five monarchs of the House of Tudor. She reigned as the Queen of England and Ireland from 1558 until her death in 1603

The Great Plague, which killed over 100,000 people in London in 1665, was a true calamity. The metropolis, which had swelled to almost a half-million people and was primarily housed in wooden constructions, was reduced to ashes once more in the Great Fire of London a year later. Many famous buildings were built in the aftermath of the fire, including Buckingham Palace and St. Paul's Cathedral.

the Great Plague

In just 18 months, the Great Plague killed an estimated 100,000 people—nearly a fourth of London's population. The plague was caused by the bacteria Yersinia pestis, which is spread by the bite of a human flea or louse.

Great Fire Of London

The Great Fire of London was a huge fire that raced across central London from Sunday, September 2 to Thursday, September 6, 1666. The fire ravaged the medieval City of London, which was located within the old Roman city walls.

The Bank of England was established in 1694 and was led by Huguenot John Houblon, who helped transform London into a global financial powerhouse. By 1840, the city had swelled to 2 million residents, many of whom were crammed into filthy hovels, contributing to cholera and other illness epidemics.

Hugenot John Houblon

From 1694 to 1697, he was the first Governor of the Bank of England.

London was well established as the prestigious seat of the vast British Empire during Queen Victoria's reign, and while Big Ben rose above the city in 1859, the London Underground, the world's first subterranean railway, opened in 1863. In 1888, however, Jack the Ripper stalked the city's women in the shadows of the great metropolis, killing at least five in one of history's most notorious murder sprees.

Queen Victoria

From June 20, 1837, to her death in 1901, Victoria reigned as Queen of the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland. Her reign of 63 years and seven months, known as the Victorian era, was the longest of any British monarch before her.

Jack The Ripper

In 1888, Jack the Ripper was an unnamed serial killer who preyed on the poor in and around London's Whitechapel district. The killer was dubbed the Whitechapel Murderer and Leather Apron in both criminal case files and contemporary journalistic reporting.

In World War I, air raids killed about 2,300 people in London, and the city was bombed mercilessly by the German Luftwaffe during the Battle of Britain in World War II, killing about 30,000 people.

German Bombing of London WW1

The aerial bombing campaign launched by Germany against Great Britain during World War I, with London as its principal target, was the world's first prolonged strategic bombing campaign. Airships, bomber planes, and seaplanes were used in these operations, which lasted from December 1914 to August 1918 and resulted in nearly 5,000 casualties.

WW2 Bombing of London

During World War II, the Blitz was a German bombing campaign against the United Kingdom in 1940 and 1941. The name was coined by the British press and is derived from the German word "Blitzkrieg," which means "lightning war."

Thousands of Londoners died during and after the Great Smog of 1952, which caused immeasurable suffering to the city's residents. In 2005, a terrorist attack on London's public transportation system killed 56 people. However, the city has continued to grow and prosper, hosting the 2012 Olympic Games and establishing itself as Europe's leading cultural and financial center.

Great Smog

The Great Smog of London, also known as the Great Smog of 1952, was a significant air pollution event that occurred in December 1952 in London, England.

London 2005 terror attack

The 7/7 London bombings were a series of four coordinated Islamist suicide attacks in London, England, on July 7, 2005, that targeted passengers using the city's public transportation system during rush hour.

Four attackers exploded three homemade bombs on London Underground trains across the city and a fourth on a double-decker bus in Tavistock Square in short succession.

2012 Olympics

The 2012 Summer Olympics were an international multi-sport event held in London, United Kingdom, from July 27 to August 12, 2012. The group stage of the women's football tournament began on July 25 at the Millennium Stadium in Cardiff, followed by the opening ceremony on July 27.
